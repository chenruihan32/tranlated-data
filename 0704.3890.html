Abstract

We introduce a distributed algorithm for clock synchronization in sensor networks. Our algorithm assumes that nodes in the network only know their immediate neighborhoods and an upper bound on the network’s diameter. Clock-synchronization messages are only sent as part of the communication, assumed reasonably frequent, that already takes place among nodes. The algorithm has the gradient property of [2], achieving an O​(1)𝑂1O(1) worst-case skew between the logical clocks of neighbors. As in the case of [3, 8], the algorithm’s actions are such that no constant lower bound exists on the rate at which logical clocks progress in time, and for this reason the lower bound of [2, 5] that forbids constant skew between neighbors does not apply.


Keywords: Distributed computing, Sensor networks, Clock synchronization, Gradient property in clock synchronization.

So we maintain the assumption, in part, but strive to reduce communication requirements as much as possible.

3 Worst-case clock skews

Step 2(b), with c=(1+ρ^)​D+1𝑐1^𝜌𝐷1c=(1+\hat{\rho})\sqrt{D+1}, is the essence of the algorithm in [4]. The reason why that algorithm guarantees a maximum skew of O​(D)𝑂𝐷O(\sqrt{D}) between the logical clocks of neighbors in G𝐺G is intimately related to this particular choice for c𝑐c and to how this choice relates to the worst-case skew between any two nodes, which is always less than (1+ρ^)​D+11^𝜌𝐷1(1+\hat{\rho})D+1. There are other factors involved, but this one is crucial and a closer examination of [4] reveals that choosing c𝑐c to be O​(1)𝑂1O(1), for example, disrupts the clocks’ gradient property.

Considered within the assumptions of our model, the problem with letting c𝑐c be O​(1)𝑂1O(1) in Step 2(b) is that a length-O​(D)𝑂𝐷O(D) wait chain may occur in G𝐺G in which each node finds out that its logical clock is ahead of the next node’s by at least c𝑐c. In this chain, the node whose logical clock is ahead of all others’ may dart still farther ahead unchecked for an O​(D)𝑂𝐷O(D) amount of time, which will then be the worst-case skew between neighbors. So, in order to accommodate the possibility of a constant value for c𝑐c along with the gradient property for some f𝑓f, a mechanism is needed to slow down the progress of logical clocks that are ahead of others by c𝑐c or more. This is what Step 2(a) does, provided c≤(1+ρ^)​d𝑐1^𝜌𝑑c\leq(1+\hat{\rho})d, as we assume henceforth. As we demonstrate shortly, an f𝑓f is achieved that implies constant skew between the logical clocks of neighbors.

4 Discussion

In [2], and also in [5] for the specific case of assumptions (i) and (ii), it is proven that f​(di​j)𝑓subscript𝑑𝑖𝑗f(d_{ij}) is Ω​(di​j+log⁡D/log⁡log⁡D)Ωsubscript𝑑𝑖𝑗𝐷𝐷\Omega(d_{ij}+\log D/\log\log D). Such a property would be seriously at odds with our claim of an O​(1)𝑂1O(1) worst-case skew between neighbors, so in this section we discuss its relation to our algorithm. Specifically, we demonstrate that our approach admits no constant lower bound on d​Li​(t)/d​t𝑑subscript𝐿𝑖𝑡𝑑𝑡dL_{i}(t)/dt that holds for all i𝑖i and all t𝑡t, so the lower bound on f​(di​j)𝑓subscript𝑑𝑖𝑗f(d_{ij}) does not hold.

5 Concluding remarks

Our algorithm’s Step 2 embodies two competing trends in its two parts (a) and (b). The aim of part (a) is to slow down nodes whose logical clocks are ahead of any of their neighbors’ by c𝑐c or more. Part (b), on the other hand, forces a node’s logical clock to move ahead toward its neighbors’ whenever possible. Both trends are fundamental to the algorithm’s proper operation. Without Step 2(a), the O​(1)𝑂1O(1) worst-case skew between neighbors would be unachievable; without Step 2(b), the presence of a single slow-moving hardware clock would slow down all nodes’ logical clocks, turning them into poor approximations of real time.

One relevant open question at this point is how the two trends balance each other, both in theory and in practice. Our algorithm relies strongly on the possibility of altering, in Step 2(a), the rates at which nodes’ logical clocks follow their hardware clocks. Even though there is a clear provision for such rates to return to their original value of 111 whenever safe, further investigation is needed to clarify their most important properties. One of these concerns the duration of the periods during which the rates get reduced. Another is related to how rate reduction affects the logical clocks’ main purpose, which is to track the progress of real time in as synchronized a way as possible.
