Abstract

1 Introduction

Optimization for multiple conflicting objectives results in more than one optimal solutions (known as Pareto-optimal solutions). Although one of these solutions is to be chosen at the end, the recent trend in evolutionary and classical multi-objective optimization studies have focused on approximating the set of Pareto-optimal solutions. However, to assess the quality of Pareto approximation set, special measures are neededÂ [1].

Hypervolume indicator is a commonly accepted quality measure for comparing approximation set generated by multi-objective optimizers. The indicator measures the hypervolume of the dominated portion of the objective space by Pareto approximation set and has received more and more attention in recent yearsÂ [2, 3, 1, 4].

There have been some studies that discuss the issue of fast hypervolume calculationÂ [5, 6, 7, 8]. These algorithms partition the covered space into many cuboid-shaped regions, within which the approach considering the dominated hypervolume as a special case of Kleeâ€™s measure problem is regarded as the current best one. This approachÂ [8] adopts orthogonal partition tree which requires Oâ€‹(nd/2)ğ‘‚superscriptğ‘›ğ‘‘2O(n^{d/2}) storage and streaming variantÂ [9]. Conceptual simplification of the implementation are concerned and thus the algorithm achieves an upper bound of Oâ€‹(nâ€‹logâ¡n+nd/2)ğ‘‚ğ‘›ğ‘›superscriptğ‘›ğ‘‘2O(n\log{n}+n^{d/2}) for the hypervolume calculation. Ignoring the running time of sorting the points according to the dğ‘‘d-th dimension, Oâ€‹(nâ€‹logâ¡n)ğ‘‚ğ‘›ğ‘›O(n\log{n}), the running time of this approach is exponential of the dimension of space dğ‘‘d.

This paper develops novel heuristics for the calculation of hypervolume indicator. Special technologies are applied and the novel approach yields upper bound of Oâ€‹((d2)n)ğ‘‚superscriptğ‘‘2ğ‘›O((\frac{d}{2})^{n}) runtime and consumes Oâ€‹(dâ€‹n2)ğ‘‚ğ‘‘superscriptğ‘›2O(dn^{2}) storage. The paper is organized as follows. In the next section, the hypervolume indicator is defined, and some background on its calculation is provided. Then, an algorithm is proposed which uses the so-called vertex-splitting technology to reduce the hypervolume. The complexities of the proposed algorithm are analyzed in SectionÂ 4. The last section concludes this paper with an open problem.

3 The proposed algorithm

In other works, e.g. the work of Beume and RudolphÂ [8], the hyper-cuboid in dğ‘‘d-dimensional space are partitioned into child hyper-cuboids along the dğ‘‘d-th dimension and then all these child hypervolumes are gathered together by the inclusion-exclusion principleÂ [10].

5 Conclusions

A fast algorithm to calculate the hypervolume indicator of Pareto approximation set is proposed. In the novel algorithm, the hyper-cuboid bounded by non-comparable points and the reference point is partitioned into many child hyper-cuboids along the carefully chosen splitting reference point at each dimension. The proposed approach is very different to the technique used in other works where the whole dğ‘‘d-dimensional volume is calculated by computing the (dâˆ’1)ğ‘‘1(d-1)-dimensional volume along the dimension dğ‘‘d. Such difference results in very different time bounds, namely Oâ€‹((d2)n)ğ‘‚superscriptğ‘‘2ğ‘›O((\frac{d}{2})^{n}) for our work and Oâ€‹(nd2)ğ‘‚superscriptğ‘›ğ‘‘2O(n^{\frac{d}{2}}) for the best previous result. Neither kind of technique can exceed the other completely and each has his strong point. Additionally, the amount of storage used by our algorithm is only Oâ€‹(dâ€‹n2)ğ‘‚ğ‘‘superscriptğ‘›2O(dn^{2}) even no special technique is developed to reduce the space complexity.

As the context has mentioned, it is very important to choose appropriate splitting reference point for our algorithm. Well selected point can reduce number of points in separated parts and thus cut down the whole runtime. We do not know whether the strategy adopted in this paper is optimal or near optimal. Further investigations should be worked on.
